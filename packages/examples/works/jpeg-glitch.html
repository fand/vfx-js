The web has never felt this solid.

After two decades of broken links and disappearing pages, the internet finally feels permanent — dependable, unbreakable, here to stay.

By Eleanor Vance · Senior Technology Correspondent
8 min read

For most of its life the web was a fragile thing. A page you read on Monday might be a 404 by Friday; a service you trusted could vanish with a single acquisition. We learned to expect impermanence.

That era, observers argue, is quietly ending. Archives are deeper, formats are stabler, and the rendering pipeline that turns markup into pixels has rarely been more dependable. What you load today, the thinking goes, will look the same tomorrow.

"There is a new confidence in the medium," one engineer told us this week. "Nothing falls apart anymore. It just works." Whether that confidence is earned — or merely the calm before something gives way — is the question this column keeps returning to.
